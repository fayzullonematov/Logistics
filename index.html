About the Major

Logistics is the part of supply chain management that deals with the efficient forward and reverse flow of goods, services, and related information from the point of origin to the point of consumption according to the needs of customers. Logistics management is a component that holds the supply chain together. The resources managed in logistics may include tangible goods such as materials, equipment, and supplies, as well as food and other edible items. Military logistics is concerned with maintaining army supply lines with food, armaments, ammunition, and spare parts, apart from the transportation of troops themselves. Meanwhile, civil logistics deals with acquiring, moving, and storing raw materials, semi-finished goods, and finished goods. For organisations that provide garbage collection, mail deliveries, public utilities, and after-sales services, logistical problems must be addressed.

In university, students explore both the analytical and strategic dimensions of logistics, learning to design efficient networks, reduce costs, and enhance sustainability in transportation and distribution systems.

History of Logistics

The term “logistics” comes from the Greek word logistikos, meaning “skilled in calculating.” Its origins can be traced back to ancient military campaigns, where the efficient movement of troops, weapons, and supplies often determined victory or defeat. Commanders like Alexander the Great and Julius Caesar relied on precise planning and resource management long before the term itself was formalized. As empires expanded, logistics evolved into a science of coordination — ensuring that armies, merchants, and explorers could reach distant territories without exhausting their supplies.

During the Industrial Revolution, logistics entered a new era. Railroads, steamships, and later automobiles transformed the speed and scale of distribution, linking factories to global markets. By the 20th century, logistics had expanded far beyond the battlefield into the realms of business, production, and international trade. The emergence of containerization in the 1950s revolutionized shipping, while advances in computing and data analysis introduced new possibilities for optimization and tracking.

Types of Logistics

Inbound Logistics

Focuses on the transportation and storage of raw materials and components coming into a company’s production process. It ensures a seamless supply of materials from suppliers to manufacturers.

Outbound Logistics

Deals with the movement of finished products from a company to the customer. It covers warehousing, packaging, distribution, and delivery.

Reverse Logistics

Manages the flow of returned goods, recycling, and waste disposal. It’s a critical part of sustainability and circular economy initiatives.

Third-Party Logistics (3PL)

Involves outsourcing logistics operations to specialized providers. These partners handle transportation, warehousing, and distribution so companies can focus on core activities.

Global Logistics

Centers on the management of international trade routes, customs, and cross-border regulations to maintain the global supply chain.

Green Logistics

Prioritizes environmentally friendly solutions — minimizing carbon footprints, using renewable energy, and promoting sustainable practices.
